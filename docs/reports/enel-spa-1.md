## Assessment

Enel's claim to be "driving the transition to a more sustainable energy system" through "accelerating renewable energy production" and "decarbonization strategy" is **materially contradicted by verified evidence of active fossil fuel expansion** across three fuel types.

### Critical Contradictions

**Coal Expansion (Confidence 0.90):** The Urgewald Global Coal Exit List documents that Enel is actively expanding coal capacity with 15 Mtpa mining expansion and 2.4 GW coal power generation capacity expansion. This is not legacy asset phase-out—it is forward expansion. The GCEL is used by 400+ financial institutions (GFANZ, PAII) as the institutional coal-screening standard. Any company simultaneously claiming energy transition while actively expanding coal capacity fails the foundational test of transition credibility. This finding is the lead greenwashing indicator.

**LNG Infrastructure Expansion (Confidence 0.85):** Enel is actively developing the Porto Empedocle LNG Terminal in Italy. Gas import infrastructure has a 40+ year operational lifespan and locks in long-term fossil fuel demand. This is not a retirement or phase-out; it is expansion. A net-zero or "decarbonization" claim is directly contradicted by investment in gas infrastructure that will operate beyond 2060.

**Oil & Gas Production (Confidence 0.72):** Enel operates 12 oil and gas extraction fields across 4 countries. 11 fields are currently producing; 1 field (Tihalatine South, Algeria) is in pre-FID stage and likely to enter development. Continued E&P investment contradicts explicit "decarbonization" claims.

### Supporting Evidence (Partial Mitigation)

**Regulated Emissions Reduction:** EPRTR data shows genuine 75% reduction in regulated industrial emissions from 2007 (31.97 Mt) to 2024 (8.07 Mt), with consistent year-on-year decline from 2020–2024. This represents substantive progress in legacy thermal asset retirement.

**Investor and Third-Party Assessments:** CA100+ rates Enel as having Net Zero Ambition and Capex Alignment aligned to Paris targets (2025). TPI rates the company as Management Quality Level 4 (Strategic) with 2050 pathway "Below 2°C." These assessments suggest strong stated climate governance and strategy.

**Coal Unit Retirement:** GCPT records 31 coal units retired or cancelled; only 7 remain operating (863 MW). This demonstrates meaningful historical coal phase-out in legacy markets.

### The Transition-Gap Pattern

Enel achieves its 75% regulated emissions reduction **in legacy thermal generation assets in regulated EU/regulated markets** (covered by EPRTR and historical coal retirement). Simultaneously, it is **expanding fossil fuel exposure in capital-intensive long-life assets** (coal mining, LNG terminals, oil & gas fields) likely located in less-regulated jurisdictions or with longer investment horizons. This is a classic transition-gap greenwashing pattern: the company is simultaneously retiring old coal plants while expanding new coal, gas, and oil projects.

### Substantiation Failure

The claim lacks the specificity required under Article 3(3)(a) of the Green Claims Directive. It does not state:
- Baseline year
- Scope (Scope 1/2/3)
- Quantified renewable production targets with timelines
- Absolute or intensity-based reduction metrics
- Decarbonization pathway timeline

The claim is aspirational language without measurable baselines or methodology disclosure.

### Lobbying Alignment

InfluenceMap score B- (neutral/mixed) and absence of A/A+/B+ ratings indicate the company does not hold demonstrable climate-leadership engagement positions in EU policy forums. No active climate policy obstruction was detected. This gap prevents **CONFIRMED_GREENWASHING** classification but is notable for a company claiming climate leadership.

## Key Finding

Enel is simultaneously achieving genuine 75% reductions in regulated coal and thermal emissions while actively expanding coal mining capacity (15 Mtpa), LNG terminals (Porto Empedocle), and oil & gas extraction (11 producing fields, 1 in pre-FID development). This transition-gap pattern—retiring legacy assets while expanding new fossil infrastructure—directly contradicts the claim to be "driving the transition to a more sustainable energy system." The company's historical emissions progress in EU-regulated plants is verified and real, but does not extend to forward capital allocation in fossil fuel expansion.
