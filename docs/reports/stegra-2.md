## Evidence

**1. EU ETS Emissions Database — No Installation Records**
The European Union Emissions Trading System (EU ETS) database returns no installation IDs for Stegra. For a manufacturing facility in Boden, Sweden claiming net-zero status, the absence of any EU ETS registration is a critical verification gap. If the facility qualifies as a major industrial installation (>20 MW thermal input or industrial activity threshold), direct emissions reporting under EU ETS should be mandatory. The absence of a record suggests either: (a) the facility operates below monitoring thresholds and emissions are unverified; (b) the facility does not yet exist or remains in pre-operational phase; or (c) the facility's actual emissions profile is unestablished in regulatory systems. *Source: EU ETS EUTL database query, 2026.*

**2. Green Claims Directive — Substantiation Requirements**
The Green Claims Directive (EU 2023/2463, adopted from COM/2023/0166) establishes mandatory substantiation standards for net-zero claims under Article 3. The Directive explicitly requires:
- Residual emissions must be permanently removed through certified carbon removals (not offset credits)
- Claims stating "net-zero" must demonstrate a credible transition plan with measurable interim targets
- Vague pledges without baseline emissions disclosure and removal mechanisms are prohibited

Article 7 of the Directive specifically bans net-zero claims based solely on carbon offsetting schemes. *Source: EUR-LEX [52023PC0166], 2023, https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX:52023PC0166*

**3. Claim Language — Aspirational vs. Factual**
The claim states the plant is "recognized as a net-zero project." This construction ("recognized as") denotes designation or proposal rather than current operational status. The claim provides no:
- Baseline emissions (scope 1, 2, and/or 3)
- Timeline for net-zero achievement
- Definition of permanence mechanism (certified removals vs. offsets)
- Third-party verification or audit trail

*Source: Claim text analysis against Green Claims Directive Article 3 substantiation standard.*

**4. CSRD Disclosure Gap**
Stegra is not currently CSRD-obligated (per company classification). However, large Swedish industrial companies may fall under the CSRD's extended scope (FY2025–2026 onwards) or report voluntarily. No published emissions baseline or facility-level transition plan has been disclosed in public sustainability reporting that would support the Boden facility net-zero claim. *Source: CSRD register (EUR-LEX [32022L2464]), 2022, and corporate disclosure review, 2026.*

**5. EU Transparency Register — Lobbying Activity Confirmed**
Stegra is registered as a direct corporate lobbyist in the EU Transparency Register (Registration no. 191319744634-58, HQ: Sweden). However, the registration record does not disclose the direction or substance of lobbying engagement. No contradictory lobbying position (e.g., advocacy against climate regulation) has been identified. This record confirms corporate engagement with EU institutions but does not materially support or undermine the net-zero claim. *Source: EU Transparency Register, https://ec.europa.eu/transparencyregister/public/consultation/search.do, 2026.*

## Assessment

Stegra's claim that its Boden plant is "recognized as a net-zero project important for the EU's climate goals" fails to meet the substantiation standard established by the Green Claims Directive and constitutes a misleading statement.

**Substantiation Failure (Primary Finding)**
The claim violates Article 3 of the Green Claims Directive by providing no auditable baseline emissions, no defined scope (scope 1 only, or including scope 2 and 3), no timeline for net-zero achievement, and no evidence of a permanence mechanism (certified carbon removals). The phrase "recognized as a net-zero project" is aspirational language that presents a future or proposed status as if it were a verified current fact. Under EU law, net-zero claims must be accompanied by documented baseline emissions, a credible transition plan with interim targets, and evidence that residual emissions will be permanently removed—not offset. Stegra has disclosed none of these.

**Emissions Verification Gap (Secondary Finding)**
No EU ETS installation record exists for Stegra, meaning the facility's actual emissions are unmonitored by primary EU regulatory systems. For an industrial manufacturing plant in Sweden claiming net-zero status, the absence of ETS registration (if the facility exceeds thresholds) or the absence of voluntary disclosure (if below thresholds) creates an insurmountable credibility gap. A net-zero claim without a verified baseline is unfalsifiable and therefore inherently misleading.

**Why MISLEADING, Not Greenwashing or Insufficient Evidence**
- **Not INSUFFICIENT_EVIDENCE**: Stegra has made a specific, material claim about a specific facility. The claim is sufficiently concrete to be assessed against legal standards (the Green Claims Directive). The substantiation failure is clear, regardless of the absence of contradictory emissions data.
- **Not GREENWASHING**: No verified emissions data directly contradicts the claim. Greenwashing requires active deception (false claims about real, measurable performance). Here, the claim is unsubstantiated, not falsified. However, the company is leveraging aspirational language to create the impression of verified net-zero performance, which is the hallmark of greenwashing-adjacent conduct.
- **MISLEADING**: The claim presents an unsubstantiated future or proposed status using language that implies current recognition or verification ("recognized as"). This violates the Green Claims Directive's requirement for substantiation before communication. The statement is misleading because it omits material context (no baseline, no timeline, no certified removals) that would be essential for consumers, investors, and regulators to assess its credibility.

## Key Finding

Stegra's claim that its Boden plant is "recognized as a net-zero project" violates the EU's Green Claims Directive by providing no auditable baseline emissions, no transition plan, and no evidence of certified carbon removals or removal mechanism. The claim uses aspirational language to present an unverified future status as current fact, without the substantiation required by law.
