### Evidence

**[1] Active coal expansion contradicts net-zero pledge**
The Urgewald Global Coal Exit List (GCEL) lists Glencore plc as actively expanding coal capacity at 100 million tonnes per annum (Mtpa), with both mining expansion status and power expansion status recorded as affirmative. Glencore is the world's largest coal exporter. The GCEL is used as the standard coal screen by more than 400 financial institutions including members of GFANZ and PAII. This documented, forward-looking expansion is directly contradictory to a credible net-zero-by-2050 industrial CO2e ambition.
*Urgewald Global Coal Exit List, data year not specified, https://www.urgewald.org/en/themen/global-coal-exit-list*

**[2] Regulatory scrutiny: CMA investigation (open)**
The UK Competition and Markets Authority (CMA) placed Glencore under scrutiny as part of its 2023 investigation into green claims by fossil fuel producers. The CMA flagged Glencore's net-zero commitment while continuing coal mining expansion as potentially misleading under UK consumer protection law. This is an active investigation; no binding ruling has been issued. It is recorded here for evidential weight, not as a concluded finding.
*CMA Greenwashing Investigation Records, 2023, https://www.gov.uk/cma-cases/greenwashing-claims*

**[3] EU Transparency Register: active EU lobbying confirmed**
Glencore plc is registered as an active direct corporate lobbyist with the EU Transparency Register (registration number 869729025581-78, HQ: Switzerland). Registration alone does not indicate the direction of lobbying. Cross-referenced with LobbyMap data (see [4]) for lobbying position assessment.
*EU Transparency Register, data year not specified, https://ec.europa.eu/transparencyregister/public/consultation/search.do*

**[4] LobbyMap score: obstructive climate policy engagement**
LobbyMap rates Glencore plc's climate policy engagement as D+ (Obstructive). This classification indicates the company actively opposes or delays climate legislation. Per the Prasine scoring framework, a D or D+ LobbyMap rating independently confirms greenwashing when held concurrently with a net-zero claim: lobbying to obstruct the policy environment that the company simultaneously cites as a precondition for its own net-zero delivery is an irreconcilable contradiction.
*LobbyMap climate policy engagement database, data year not specified, https://lobbymap.org/*

**[5] EmpCo Directive: offset-reliant net-zero claims blacklisted**
The EmpCo Directive (EU 2024/825), in force since March 2024, amends Annex I of the Unfair Commercial Practices Directive to blacklist environmental claims that are not substantiated by recognised scientific evidence. Net-zero claims must demonstrate that residual emissions will be addressed through certified permanent carbon removals — not offset credits. Generic net-zero pledges without a credible and verifiable transition plan are automatically classified as unfair commercial practices under the amended Annex I.
*EUR-Lex, [32024L0825] EmpCo Directive (EU 2024/825), 2024, https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX:32024L0825*

**[6] CSRD: disclosure obligation and applicability to Glencore**
The Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD, 2022/2464) requires mandatory ESRS E1 emissions disclosures from large companies on EU-regulated markets. Glencore plc is incorporated in Jersey and listed on the London Stock Exchange and Johannesburg Stock Exchange — neither is an EU-regulated market, so the direct FY2024 mandate does not apply. The route that applies to non-EU parent companies with net turnover exceeding €150M in the EU (which Glencore meets) begins in FY2028, with first reports due 2029. If Glencore's EU subsidiaries independently meet the large-company threshold, those entities may have earlier obligations, but no group-level CSRD disclosure currently exists or is required for FY2024. A net-zero claim materially inconsistent with future mandatory disclosures will constitute a legal inconsistency once reporting begins.
*EUR-Lex, [32022L2464] CSRD, 2022, https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX:32022L2464*

**[7] Source document: offset reliance and substantiation deficiencies confirmed**
Glencore's own sustainability disclosure confirms the assessed claim and discloses its substantiation basis. The document records a 2019 baseline and interim reduction targets of 15% by 2026, 25% by 2030, and 50% by 2035. Critically, the document states that the company "supports the use of carbon credits to offset residual emissions" and will "consider the business case for both generating and utilising carbon offsets" to meet the 2050 target. This reliance on carbon offset credits — rather than certified permanent removal mechanisms such as direct air capture (DAC), bioenergy with carbon capture and storage (BECCS), or biochar — fails the EmpCo substantiation standard. The document additionally lacks: actual emissions data for any recent year; a 2040 interim target; an explicit abatement-versus-removal split; and a verified transition plan demonstrating how net-zero will be achieved beyond offsets. The conditionality phrase "subject to a supportive policy environment" is a material qualifier that renders the commitment non-binding.
*Glencore plc Sustainability Disclosure (self-reported), data year not specified, https://www.glencore.com/sustainability/esg-a-z/climate-change*

**[8] TPI assessment: carbon performance pathway not assessed**

### Regulatory Exposure

**(a) EmpCo Directive (EU 2024/825, in force March 2024)**

This claim violates the EmpCo Directive. Glencore's own disclosure confirms the company "supports the use of carbon credits to offset residual emissions" and will "consider the business case for both generating and utilising carbon offsets." Under the amended UCPD Annex I, as codified by EU 2024/825, net-zero claims that rely on carbon offset credits rather than certified permanent removal mechanisms are automatically blacklisted as unfair commercial practices. EUR-Lex record [32024L0825] confirms this standard explicitly. The claim additionally fails the mandatory substantiation requirement: it lacks actual recent emissions data, a 2040 interim target, an abatement-versus-removal split, and a verified transition plan. The conditionality clause ("subject to a supportive policy environment") further undermines substantiation by rendering the commitment contingent rather than binding.

Under UCPD Article 11 (as amended by the EmpCo Directive, EU 2024/825), this claim is subject to enforcement by national consumer protection authorities. Complaints may be filed with the relevant national consumer protection authority in each EU Member State where Glencore's sustainability communications are accessible to consumers or investors — including, but not limited to, the Autorité de la concurrence et de la consommation (France), the Autorità Garante della Concorrenza e del Mercato (Italy), and the Autoridad de Competencia y Regulación (Spain), as well as the European Consumer Organisation (BEUC) for cross-border referrals. Given Glencore's primary listing in the United Kingdom, complaints may also be filed with the CMA, which has an open investigation into this company.

**(b) Green Claims Directive (enforcement begins September 2026)**

The Green Claims Directive introduces mandatory pre-market independent verification of environmental claims. This claim would not pass GCD scrutiny. The absence of certified removal mechanisms, the offset-credit reliance, the missing 2040 interim target, and the non-binding conditionality clause each constitute independent grounds for verification failure. With GCD enforcement commencing September 2026, Glencore's current disclosure framework requires material restructuring to avoid enforcement exposure.
