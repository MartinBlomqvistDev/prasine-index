### Assessment

Danone's 2050 net-zero pledge is substantively inadequate under mandatory EU law and lacks material interim accountability mechanisms, earning a **MISLEADING** classification rather than either insufficient data or confirmed greenwashing.

**Core Finding: Regulatory Substantiation Failure**

The Green Claims Directive (Article 3, enforceable 2026) explicitly requires that net-zero claims be supported by:
- A baseline of absolute emissions by scope (Scope 1, 2, 3)
- Explicit interim reduction targets (typically 2030/2035)
- A credible carbon removal strategy naming certified offset mechanisms, not generic offsetting

Danone's published claim statement contains none of these elements. The company's sustainability webpage presents a bare 2050 aspiration with no quantified baseline, no disclosed interim milestones, and no methodology for residual emissions removal. Under the Directive's substantiation test, this claim is **materially deficient**—not merely unverified, but affirmatively non-compliant with Article 3(1).

**Secondary Finding: Absence of Science-Based Target Validation**

SBTi records show no validation status for Danone's net-zero target. This is material because:
- SBTi is the international standard for assessing whether interim targets (e.g., 2030 reduction) are consistent with 1.5°C warming limits
- A 2050 net-zero pledge without SBTi-validated near-term targets lacks physical credibility; the company may be on a 2°C or higher pathway
- For a food-beverage multinational with significant Scope 3 agricultural supply chain emissions, a single 2050 endpoint without intermediate checkpoints is standard greenwashing architecture—technically compliant with net-zero discourse but strategically timed to avoid near-term accountability

TPI's 2022 assessment similarly shows "N/A" for climate pathway validation, indicating no independent third-party confirmation that Danone's stated targets align with 1.5°C science.

**Tertiary Finding: Critical Emissions Data Gap**

Danone has no registered EU ETS installation IDs despite operating food and beverage facilities across Europe. The only available verified emissions record is a single 2008 E-PRTR data point (188 t CO2e), which is:
- 18 years old
- Insufficient to establish baseline or trend
- Likely unrepresentative of current Scope 1 emissions

This gap prevents verification that Danone's claimed net-zero pathway is consistent with its actual baseline emissions. For a company claiming absolute net-zero by 2050, the absence of current Scope 1/2 emissions verification is a critical failure. The company's CSRD obligation (as a large EU-listed entity with >500 employees) requires Scope 1, 2, and 3 disclosure; if Danone's most recent CSRD filing contradicts or fails to align with the stated 2050 target, this constitutes a material inconsistency under the Directive.

**Supporting Evidence Context**

CA100+ (2025) validates that Danone has articulated net-zero ambition and is partially operationalizing it through capital allocation. However, CA100+ assessment—conducted at investor confidence level 0.65—is weaker than SBTi validation and does not substitute for science-based interim target credibility. CA100+ confirms directional alignment; it does not confirm regulatory compliance or 1.5°C pathway alignment.

InfluenceMap's B- engagement score suggests Danone neither actively opposes climate legislation nor champions it. No evidence of lobbying *against* climate regulation is detected. However, this neutral positioning is incongruent with a credible net-zero claim; companies with genuine net-zero transition strategies typically advocate for stronger carbon pricing and climate policy. The B- rating does not trigger a greenwashing signal, but it undercuts the claim's strategic authenticity.

**Historical Pattern**

Analysis of Danone's prior 24 assessed claims yields an average score of 47.9 (MISLEADING band), indicating a pattern of overstated or weakly substantiated climate commitments. This new 2050 net-zero pledge—unsubstantiated by interim targets, baseline emissions, or science-based validation—continues this trajectory. The claim is not a novel position but consistent with the company's demonstrated greenwashing profile.

**Why MISLEADING, Not GREENWASHING or INSUFFICIENT_EVIDENCE**

- **Not INSUFFICIENT_EVIDENCE (21–45):** The claim is not merely unverified; it is *affirmatively non-compliant* with mandatory EU law (Green Claims Directive Article 3). Under the substantiation standard, absence of baseline, interim targets, and carbon removal methodology is a regulatory-grade failure, not a data gap. The company's own disclosure obligation (CSRD) would require this information if disclosed—omission is therefore strategic.

- **Not GREENWASHING (61–80):** Verified emissions data sufficient to directly contradict the claim is unavailable. The 2008 E-PRTR datum is too old and limited. CA100+ provides partial supporting evidence. The claim is therefore not *materially contradicted* by available data—it is *inadequately substantiated* and strategically timed to present an aspiration as a credible commitment without disclosure of interim accountability or baseline.

- **MISLEADING (46–60) is calibrated:** The claim exaggerates through strategic omission. By presenting a 2050 net-zero pledge without disclosed baseline, interim targets, SBTi validation, or carbon removal methodology, Danone creates the impression of a credible long-term commitment while deferring concrete accountability to 2050—beyond the operational horizon of current management. This is the definitional structure of MISLEADING: a claim not yet falsified but materially overstating certainty and actionability through absence of material qualifications.
