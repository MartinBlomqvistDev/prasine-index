### Evidence

**[1] EmpCo Directive (EU 2024/825) — Amended UCPD Annex I**
The European Commission's Green Claims Directive, in force since March 2024, explicitly blacklists carbon-neutrality claims that rely primarily on offset or removal schemes without verified absolute emission reductions across the company's full lifecycle. The amended UCPD Annex I states that carbon-neutral claims must be substantiated by documented, independently verified reductions in the trader's own operations, not by purchase of removal credits alone. Direct air capture (DAC) is classified as a removal technology and therefore a form of offsetting under this framework.
*Source: EUR_LEX legislative record [32024L0825], confidence 0.95, https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX:32024L0825*

**[2] SBTi Target Record — H&M Group**
H&M Group holds a Science-Based Targets initiative (SBTi) validated target at 1.5°C temperature class with status "Targets set." This confirms H&M has committed to interim emission reduction targets aligned with climate science. However, SBTi validation does not extend to carbon-neutrality claims or to the sufficiency of offset/removal strategies. SBTi scope covers interim reductions to 2030; it does not validate 2050 net-zero claims or the Climeworks contract specifically.
*Source: SBTi companies database, confidence 0.70, https://sciencebasedtargets.org/companies-taking-action*

**[3] EU ETS Verified Emissions Data**
H&M Group has no registered installations in the EU Emissions Trading System. This is not anomalous (fashion retailers typically own no power generation or industrial production facilities directly); however, it means no independently verified baseline emissions data is available from a mandatory EU reporting scheme. The absence of EU ETS data limits independent verification of H&M's absolute emissions trajectory—a critical gap under EmpCo Directive substantiation requirements.
*Source: EU ETS EUTL database (queried 2026-06-24), confidence 0.95 (search completeness), https://ec.europa.eu/clima/ets*

### Assessment

The factual assertion that H&M signed a multi-year contract with Climeworks in 2022 is not contradicted by available evidence. However, the claim is **misleading in its framing and context** because it lacks the mandatory disclosures now required under EU law as of March 2024.

**Substantiation Failure Under EmpCo Directive:**

H&M presents this Climeworks contract under the heading "Capturing carbon dioxide from the atmosphere" on its sustainability page, positioning it as active climate strategy. Under the EmpCo Directive (in force since March 2024), carbon-neutrality or carbon-related claims must be substantiated by:

1. **Verified baseline emissions** across scope 1, 2, and 3 — not provided in the quoted claim or linked disclosure.
2. **Documented absolute emission reductions** in the company's own operations — no quantified reduction trajectory is cited.
3. **Transparent allocation** of which emissions are reduced operationally vs. removed via DAC — not disclosed.
4. **Independent verification of DAC permanence and volume** — no certification standard or audit trail is stated.

H&M's SBTi target validates interim reductions to 2030 but does not validate the Climeworks strategy or any longer-term carbon-neutrality claim. The amended UCPD Annex I explicitly forbids claims that "rely primarily on offset or removal schemes without verified absolute emission reductions across the full product or company lifecycle." H&M's claim, as framed, presents a removal contract without disclosing the baseline against which removal efficacy can be measured.

**Regulatory Context:**

The EmpCo Directive amended UCPD Article 6 to classify such unsubstantiated removal-based neutrality claims as potentially misleading under Article 6(1), which covers claims that deceive the average consumer about environmental impact. The directive specifically targets the practice of presenting offset or removal purchases as a substitute for operational decarbonization—which is the core structure of H&M's claim as presented.

**Confidence Rationale:**

Confidence is set at 72% (not higher) because:
- The Climeworks contract itself appears verifiable (no contradicting evidence exists).
- The misleading aspect is **omission** of mandatory disclosures, not factual falsity.
- If H&M has published baseline emissions, absolute reduction data, and DAC permanence standards elsewhere on its website or in its annual sustainability report, this would partially mitigate the score.
- The EmpCo Directive is newly in force (March 2024) and this may be the company's first exposure to the requirement.

However, the substantiation gap is **clear under enforceable EU law**, which is why the verdict is MISLEADING rather than INSUFFICIENT_EVIDENCE.

### Key Finding

H&M's Climeworks contract claim violates the EmpCo Directive (in force March 2024) by presenting direct air capture removal as climate strategy without disclosing verified baseline emissions, absolute reduction progress, or independent verification of removal permanence—mandatory substantiation now required for carbon-related claims under EU law.
