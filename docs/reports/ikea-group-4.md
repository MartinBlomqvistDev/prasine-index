### The Claim
> "IWAY 6, which was introduced to suppliers in September 2020. This latest version of IWAY is a total system upgrade, that introduced improved ways of working as well as new topics, such as biodiversity and conservation of natural resources, animal welfare and an increased focus on the competence development of workers."

### Evidence

**[1] E-PRTR Regulated Industrial Emissions to Air, 2010–2024**
IKEA Group's direct regulated emissions show a long-term upward trend: 323.2 tonnes (2020) → 1,430.0 t (2021) → 1,599.5 t (2022) → 1,242.0 t (2023). The 2024 figure of 110,243.0 tonnes represents an 89-fold increase from 2023 and is flagged as a likely first-disclosure event (new facility or pollutant category entering E-PRTR scope) rather than an operational change. Excluding 2024, the 2010–2023 trajectory shows a 902% rise in regulated emissions to air. These figures measure only direct regulated pollution at IKEA facilities, not upstream supply-chain emissions (material processing, deforestation, transport), which represent IKEA's material environmental footprint as a furniture and goods retailer.
*Source: European Pollutant Release and Transfer Register (E-PRTR), https://industry.eea.europa.eu/, 2024 data year*

IKEA Group is registered as an active EU corporate lobbyist (Registration No. 774019931221-41, HQ: Netherlands). The registration confirms IKEA engages EU institutions on policy matters but does not indicate the direction or substance of that engagement.
*Source: EU Transparency Register, https://ec.europa.eu/transparencyregister/public/consultation/search.do, registration status current*

**[3] Green Claims Directive (EUR-LEX 52023PC0166) — Substantiation Standard**
The Green Claims Directive (COM/2023/0166, Article 3(1)) prohibits explicit environmental claims without substantiation by recognised scientific evidence. The Directive specifically targets claims that mislead by omission—presenting procedural changes or aspirations as material environmental improvements without demonstrable outcomes or lifecycle impact validation. Vague environmental claims without specific, measurable, or independently verified substantiation are prohibited.
*Source: EUR-LEX, https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX:52023PC0166, 2023*

### Assessment

The claim describes a procedural upgrade to IKEA's supplier code of conduct (IWAY 6), introducing new topics including biodiversity, animal welfare, conservation of natural resources, and worker competence development in September 2020. This is a factual statement about code architecture, not a future emissions or net-zero commitment. The procedural description is internally consistent with IKEA's publicly documented policies.

However, the claim fails substantiation under the Green Claims Directive standard (EUR-LEX 52023PC0166, Article 3(1)) through critical omissions:

1. **No baseline or measurable outcome**: The claim states what topics were *added* to the code but provides no evidence that suppliers have implemented these requirements, that compliance is independently verified, or that measurable biodiversity or welfare improvements have occurred. A code upgrade in procedural architecture is not evidence of environmental or social impact.

2. **No independent verification**: The claim offers no third-party audit, certification record, or supplier compliance data demonstrating that IWAY 6 biodiversity or animal welfare requirements have been operationalised or enforced across IKEA's supply chain.

3. **Absence vs. contradiction**: No evidence contradicts the procedural claim that IWAY 6 exists or includes these topics. The failure is substantiation—the claim risks misleading stakeholders into believing a code upgrade represents material environmental or welfare improvement without demonstrating that it has led to measurable outcomes.

**Direct operations emissions context**: E-PRTR data (Record [1]) shows IKEA's regulated air emissions rose 902% from 2010 to 2023 (323.2 t in 2020 to 1,242.0 t in 2023, excluding the 2024 anomaly). However, this measures only direct facility emissions, not the vastly larger upstream supply-chain footprint that IWAY 6 purports to address. The unfavorable emissions trend in direct operations does not directly contradict a supplier code claim but suggests IKEA's overall sustainability trajectory is inconsistent with aspirational messaging.

**Lobbying alignment**: InfluenceMap B-band score (Record [4]) indicates IKEA's lobbying stance is broadly supportive of environmental policy, showing no active contradiction. However, confidence is only 0.75, and the InfluenceMap record does not evaluate IKEA's specific positions on supply-chain due diligence (CSDDD), deforestation regulation, or biodiversity legislation—areas directly relevant to the IWAY 6 claim. This represents a data gap rather than a contradiction.

**SBTi status**: Absence of an SBTi net-zero target (Record [5]) indicates IKEA has not committed to independently validated, science-based emissions reduction pathways. This does not contradict the IWAY 6 claim but underscores the lack of measurable environmental commitment at corporate level.

**Historical pattern**: IKEA's prior claims (n=26) average a score of 52.6 (median: MISLEADING), suggesting a pattern of vague, aspirational sustainability messaging without robust verification. No prior biodiversity claims are recorded, so direct repetition cannot be assessed.

---

### Key Finding

IKEA's claim that IWAY 6 introduced biodiversity, animal welfare, and conservation topics to its supplier code is procedurally factual but substantiation-deficient: the company presents a code upgrade as a material improvement without providing evidence of supplier compliance, independent verification, or measurable biodiversity or welfare outcomes. This structure—procedural adoption without demonstrated impact—constitutes a Green Claims Directive substantiation failure and represents greenwashing by omission.

---

### Data Gaps

1. **IWAY 6 Supplier Compliance Data**: No independently audited record of supplier adoption rates, compliance frequency, or audit findings for IWAY 6 biodiversity or animal welfare requirements is publicly available. This gap directly limits confidence: if detailed supplier audit data exist but were not disclosed, substantiation claims strengthen; if compliance is undocumented, the greenwashing signal intensifies.

2. **Biodiversity Baseline and KPIs**: No baseline biodiversity impact measurement pre-IWAY 6 or post-2020 quantitative targets (e.g., % supply-chain farms or forests under biodiversity management) are publicly available. Without baseline and target, the claim cannot be evaluated against the Green Claims Directive's substantiation standard.

3. **Third-Party Certification Records**: No evidence of third-party biodiversity certification (e.g., FSC, Rainforest Alliance, B-Corp audits) for IKEA suppliers is provided in the claim. Absence of such records suggests either non-existence or non-disclosure.

4. **IKEA Lobbying Specificity**: InfluenceMap provides a B-band aggregate score but does not detail IKEA's specific positions on EU biodiversity policy, deforestation regulation (EUDR), or supply-chain due diligence (CSDDD). The "broadly supportive" classification does not confirm support for provisions relevant to IWAY 6 enforcement.

5. **EU ETS Registration**: No IKEA installations are registered in the EU ETS, reflecting IKEA's retail/logistics profile rather than energy-intensive manufacturing. This gap is immaterial to the biodiversity claim and does not reduce confidence.

**Impact on Confidence**: Data gaps on supplier compliance, biodiversity baselines, and independent verification directly constrain confidence from a potential 75–85% down to 58%. If IKEA published verifiable supplier compliance rates, biodiversity KPIs, or third-party audit summaries, confidence would rise to 75%+; their absence is not a contradiction but a substantiation failure.
