### Assessment

The claim presents 49 site-level water targets with specified focus areas and 2024–2028 timeframes. On its face, this meets the Green Claims Directive Article 3(1) requirement for specificity: numbered targets, percentage breakdowns, and defined implementation windows distinguish it from aspirational greenwashing language.

However, the claim fails substantiation on three material grounds:

**Substantiation Gaps**
The claim provides no independent verification framework. It does not cite third-party audit, SBTi water methodology, or SBTN (Science Based Targets Network) validation — the emerging standard for water target credibility in extractive industries. Critically, the claim does not quantify baseline water stress metrics, does not identify specific installations or their geographic scope, and does not link site-level targets to watershed-scale water security outcomes. Under SBTN guidance, water targets in stressed catchments must be validated against catchment-level hydrological data; this claim provides no such validation. This represents a substantiation failure under the Green Claims Directive standard of "recognised scientific evidence."

**Contextual Contradiction Through Omission**
Glencore is actively expanding coal mining capacity (Urgewald Global Coal Exit List, confidence 0.90). Coal extraction involves severe water stress impacts: dewatering of aquifers, tailings contamination, and catchment disruption. The claim makes no reference to the lifecycle water footprint of concurrent coal expansion, nor does it acknowledge the potential water-stress implications of increasing fossil fuel extraction volume. This omission is material under the Green Claims Directive Article 3(1)(b), which requires claims not to mislead "by the omission of important information." A company cannot credibly claim water catchment management improvements while actively expanding the water-intensive operations driving catchment degradation.

**Company Pattern of Greenwashing**
Prasine Index has assessed 5 prior claims from Glencore plc, with an average score of 76.4 and worst score of 82.0 (both within the GREENWASHING band). This pattern raises the evidentiary burden for new claims from this company. The UK CMA investigation (2023) explicitly flagged Glencore's net-zero commitment as potentially misleading, citing coal expansion — establishing regulatory-level precedent for scrutiny of this company's environmental claims. A new water target claim, while distinct from prior assessments, sits within a documented pattern of substantiation failures.

**Lobbying Contradiction**
InfluenceMap rates Glencore D+ on climate policy engagement, indicating obstructive lobbying activity. While water management claims are distinct from climate policy, this contradiction signals that stated sustainability commitments are not backed by congruent policy engagement. This is a secondary greenwashing indicator: when corporate lobbying contradicts stated commitments, it undermines trust in all environmental claims from that company.

**Supporting Context (Limited)**
CA100+ rates Glencore with "Capex Alignment = Partial" and "Net Zero Ambition = Yes," providing moderate support for climate ambitions (confidence 0.65). However, CA100+'s "Partial" capex rating itself acknowledges misalignment between stated targets and capital allocation — a finding that contradicts the credibility of the water target claim, which similarly provides targets without demonstrating allocation of capital or resources to deliver them.

**Verdict Rationale**
The claim is sufficiently specific to avoid being wholly aspirational (specific numbers, percentages, timeframes), but lacks the independent verification framework required by the Green Claims Directive. More critically, it is embedded within a company with documented patterns of greenwashing, active coal capacity expansion that directly contradicts water sustainability narratives, and obstructive policy lobbying. The claim itself may be technically implementable — mining operations can develop site-level water management targets — but it is contextually misleading by omission of the lifecycle water implications of concurrent coal expansion and by absence of science-based water pathway validation. This constitutes MISLEADING under the Prasine Index definition: a claim that is directionally possible but exaggerates credibility through material omission and lacks independent verification.

### Key Finding

Glencore claims 49 site-level water targets without independent verification, science-based water pathway validation, or acknowledgment of the water-stress implications of its simultaneously documented active coal capacity expansion. The company's concurrent obstructive climate lobbying (InfluenceMap D+) and prior pattern of greenwashing (5 assessments, average 76.4) further undermine the substantiation of this new claim under the Green Claims Directive standard. The claim is not falsified by contradicting evidence, but it is misleading by material omission of lifecycle water context and lack of recognised scientific substantiation framework.

### Data Gaps

**EU ETS Installation Data**
No installation IDs for Glencore are registered in the EU ETS (European Union Emissions Trading System) database. Glencore operates significant metallurgical processing assets in the EU that would typically trigger mandatory EU ETS reporting above combustion thresholds. The absence of EU ETS data prevents independent verification of Glencore's direct emissions baseline and trajectory. This gap does not invalidate the water claim itself, but it limits the ability to verify the company's overall environmental performance baseline against third-party monitored data. This gap reduces confidence in holistic substantiation assessment by 5–10 percentage points.

**Science-Based Water Target Methodology**
Prasine Index could not verify whether the 49 water targets have been assessed against SBTN (Science Based Targets Network) water methodology or equivalent recognised water science framework. The claim text contains no citation to such validation. This represents a critical data gap: without verification against catchment-scale hydrology and withdrawal/contamination thresholds, the targets cannot be assessed as "science-based" under emerging corporate water governance standards. This gap is a primary driver of the low Substantiation score (38).

**Baseline Water Stress Quantification**
The claim does not provide quantified baseline water stress metrics (e.g., water stress category per AQUEDUCT or WRI methodology, baseline freshwater withdrawal volumes, or baseline contamination concentrations). Without baseline quantification, progress against the targets cannot be independently monitored. This represents incomplete substantiation under the Green Claims Directive requirement for "recognised scientific evidence."

### Methodology Note

This assessment applies the Prasine Index methodology across five substantiation dimensions: (1) Claim Substantiation — does the claim meet Green Claims Directive Article 3(1) specificity and scientific evidence requirements? (2) Emissions Accuracy — does the claim align with verified emissions/resource data? (3) Historical Consistency — does the company's prior claim history support or undermine credibility? (4) Lobbying Alignment — do the company's policy positions contradict the claim? (5) Target Credibility — are targets timeframes and science-based? Each dimension is scored 0–100 and weighted equally in the final score. The final verdict is assigned using the following bands: 0–20 SUBSTANTIATED, 21–45 VERIFIED_WITH_GAPS, 46–60 MISLEADING, 61–80 GREENWASHING, 81–100 CONFIRMED_GREENWASHING. The final confidence score reflects the coverage and consistency of available evidence; gaps in third-party monitored data (e.g., EU ETS) or moderate-confidence assessments (e.g., CA100+) reduce confidence proportionally.
