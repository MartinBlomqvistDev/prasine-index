### Evidence

**[1] Green Claims Directive (COM/2023/0166)**, EU legislative record, 2023, https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=CELEX:52023PC0166  
Article 3(1) of the Green Claims Directive prohibits all explicit environmental claims unless substantiated by recognised scientific evidence. The directive explicitly forbids vague language such as aspirational statements without specific, measurable targets, timelines, or quantified baselines. Claims must cover full lifecycle environmental impact where material to the core business. Directive entered force November 2023.

### Assessment

The claim presents Öresundskraft's reuse of slag and bottom ash from what appears to be waste-to-energy or incineration operations as a circular economy initiative. However, it fails the substantiation threshold established by the Green Claims Directive for four interconnected reasons:

**1. Aspirational Language Without Measurable Commitment**

The Swedish text "försöker vi använda på bästa sätt" ("we try to use in the best way") is an expression of effort, not a specific commitment. This language does not identify:
- Quantified reuse targets (percentage of slag or ash diverted from landfill)
- Timelines for achievement
- Baseline measurement (total slag or ash generated annually)
- Performance metrics (e.g., tonnes recovered per year)

Under COM/2023/0166 Article 3(1), such aspirational framing without quantified substantiation violates the directive's core requirement that all explicit environmental claims be specific and measurable.

**2. Research-Stage Potential Presented as Substantive Practice**

The second element of the claim—"Vi bevakar också den forskning som pågår" ("We also monitor the research underway")—explicitly states that bottom ash reuse is at the research stage, not current practice. By presenting this as part of a circular economy initiative without temporal distinction, the claim exaggerates through omission: it conflates exploratory research with implemented waste recovery, misleading readers about the scope of actual reuse.

The slag reuse at NSR's facility appears operationalised; the bottom ash claim does not. The claim structure implies equivalence between these two practices, which is substantively false.

**3. Absence of Quantification and Environmental Impact Data**

No data is disclosed on:
- Annual slag generation vs. annual slag recovered for reuse
- Percentage recovery rate (slag)
- Annual bottom ash generation
- Current percentage of bottom ash deployed in asphalt applications (as opposed to landfilled or otherwise disposed)
- Environmental benefit quantification: avoided virgin gravel extraction, CO₂ avoidance from reduced quarrying, or equivalent metrics
- Timeline for bottom ash research transition to commercial-scale deployment

The Green Claims Directive requires environmental claims to substantiate the claimed benefit with "recognised scientific evidence." Unquantified reuse claims cannot be independently verified and do not meet this threshold.

**4. Omission of Lifecycle Context**

If Öresundskraft operates a waste-to-energy facility (incineration), the primary environmental footprint derives from combustion, not from slag/ash disposal. The claim focuses exclusively on secondary waste stream management without contextualising this against the core business's lifecycle emissions. This omission is material: reusing 100% of ash does not offset emissions from combustion of municipal or industrial waste.

The Green Claims Directive, Article 3(1), requires claims to cover "the full lifecycle" where material to environmental performance. A circular economy claim from an incineration operator must address whether ash reuse reduces the overall operational footprint—it does not, because the primary impact is combustion.

**5. Verification Gap Due to Regulatory Data Absence**

EU ETS query returned no installation IDs for Öresundskraft. This creates a critical verification gap:
- If Öresundskraft operates a combustion facility (implied by slag/ash generation), it should be registered under EU ETS.
- The absence of a registration prevents independent verification of annual waste inputs, emissions, and ash generation volumes.
- Without baseline data, the materiality and credibility of slag/ash reuse rates cannot be assessed.

This gap reduces confidence in the claim's accuracy and prevents third-party validation of substantiation.

**Comparative Assessment Against Company History**

Öresundskraft's prior 11 claims averaged 42.8/100, indicating a consistent pattern of substantiation gaps. The current claim follows this trajectory: it addresses a conceptually sound circular economy practice but fails to provide the quantified, measurable commitments required by EU law. This is not an outlier for the company, but a repetition of its documented disclosure pattern.

### Key Finding

Öresundskraft's circular economy claim exaggerates current practice by presenting research-stage potential (bottom ash) as substantive initiative, lacks quantified baselines or recovery percentages, and omits full-lifecycle environmental context required under the Green Claims Directive (COM/2023/0166 Article 3)—making the claim misleading despite the directional soundness of slag/ash reuse as a practice.

### Methodology Note

This assessment applied the Prasine Index methodology for evaluating claims against the Green Claims Directive (COM/2023/0166). The claim was scored on two dimensions: (1) substantiation completeness under Article 3(1) (requiring specific, measurable, lifecycle-aware claims supported by recognised evidence); (2) verification possibility against available third-party data (EU ETS, ISO standards, academic literature). 

The 52/100 score reflects a claim that is directionally possible (slag/ash reuse does occur in circular economy contexts) but fails substantiation through vagueness, omission of quantification, conflation of research-stage potential with current practice, and absence of lifecycle context. The 60% confidence reflects the verification gap created by missing EU ETS data and absence of quantified company-level disclosure; this gap prevents elevation to "clearly misleading" status (which would require contradicting verified data) and instead places the claim in the "misleading through substantiation failure" category.

Scoring rubric: 0–30 = False/Unsubstantiated (contradicted or unverifiable); 31–60 = Misleading (exaggerated, vague, or incomplete substantiation); 61–80 = Partially Substantiated (claim supported by some evidence, gaps remain); 81–100 = Well-Substantiated (claim supported by quantified, lifecycle-aware evidence with independent verification).
