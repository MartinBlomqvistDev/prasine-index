### Assessment

Ryanair's claim to power 12.5% of flights with SAF by 2030 through partnership with Trinity College Dublin is classified as **GREENWASHING** based on the following analysis:

**Primary Contradiction: Coal Expansion vs. Energy Transition Claims**

The Urgewald Global Coal Exit List confirms Ryanair Holdings plc is actively expanding coal capacity. This is a material and direct contradiction to any claim of commitment to sustainable energy transition. A company cannot credibly market itself as transitioning to sustainable aviation fuel while simultaneously expanding coal operations. This is not a data gap or interpretation dispute—it is a documented fact used by 400+ institutional investors (GFANZ, PAII) as the standard coal exposure screen. **This single finding is sufficient to classify the claim as greenwashing.**

**Pattern of Regulatory Enforcement**

The claim occurs against a documented pattern of unsubstantiated environmental marketing:
- **ASA Enforcement (2020)**: Ryanair was formally found to have made a misleading environmental claim ("lowest carbon emissions") and could not substantiate it on a comparable basis with other European airlines. This is a regulatory determination by a recognized consumer protection authority, not a disputed interpretation.
- **EC Investigation (2024)**: The European Commission specifically launched a coordinated investigation into Ryanair's environmental claims. This is active regulatory scrutiny and signals that EU authorities have identified a pattern of concern.

This claim is not the first; it is part of a systemic pattern. The company's credibility on environmental claims is compromised by prior enforcement action.

**Policy Obstruction Contradicts Public Commitments**

InfluenceMap rates Ryanair as D+ (obstructive) on climate policy engagement. The company actively opposes or delays climate legislation while publicly committing to SAF expansion. This is a core greenwashing dynamic: stated climate ambition contradicted by actual policy lobbying. A company cannot claim commitment to sustainable aviation fuel while simultaneously lobbying against the regulatory frameworks designed to enable and mandate it.

**Substantiation Failure Against Green Claims Directive Standards**

The claim lacks basic substantiation required under the Green Claims Directive:
- No baseline SAF usage disclosed (cannot assess achievement against unknown starting point)
- No measurement methodology specified (unclear whether 12.5% is by volume, energy content, or flight count)
- No interim milestones disclosed (making verification impossible before 2030)
- No evidence of secured SAF supply (supply constraints are well-documented in aviation industry)
- No offtake agreement mentioned

Under Article 3 of the Green Claims Directive proposal (COM/2023/0166), such claims must specify percentage of total energy consumption covered and the source. This claim meets none of these requirements.

**Limited Target Ambition and Credibility**

A 12.5% SAF target by 2030 is modest relative to industry expectations and regulatory momentum. The EU has proposed 2% SAF blending mandates for 2025, suggesting that 12.5% by 2030 is neither particularly ambitious nor implausible. However, credibility is further undermined by:
- No disclosed baseline (the target may be trivially easy to meet if current SAF usage is minimal)
- No binding offtake agreements mentioned
- No evidence of investment in SAF supply infrastructure

**Partial Mitigation: TPI Assessment**

The TPI assessment (2022) rates Ryanair's 2050 pathway as Paris-compatible ("Below 2 Degrees"). This provides partial mitigation and prevents a CONFIRMED_GREENWASHING classification. However, TPI assessments are backward-looking evaluations of management process and do not verify forward-looking commitments. A company can have credible long-term climate strategy while making misleading near-term claims.

**Scoring Summary**

- **CLAIM_SUBSTANTIATION (55/100)**: The claim specifies a quantified target (12.5% SAF) but lacks baseline disclosure, verification mechanism, interim milestones, and evidence of supply security. It fails to meet Green Claims Directive standards. The target is not entirely unverifiable in principle, but no methodology for verification is disclosed.

- **EMISSIONS_ACCURACY (70/100)**: While this is a future commitment rather than a past emissions reduction claim, the coal expansion documented by Urgewald creates a material contradiction. The company's credibility on emissions-related claims is compromised by both the ASA enforcement ruling and the active EC investigation.

- **HISTORICAL_CONSISTENCY (88/100)**: Strong evidence of pattern. ASA enforcement (2020) found prior claims misleading. EC investigation (2024) targets Ryanair's environmental claims. Prior average greenwashing score: 80.2. This is a repeat signal.

- **LOBBYING_ALIGNMENT (85/100)**: InfluenceMap D+ (obstructive) directly contradicts the claim of climate commitment. This is a primary greenwashing indicator but does not alone justify CONFIRMED_GREENWASHING because SAF is technically a plausible (if unproven) commitment for an airline.

- **TARGET_CREDIBILITY (65/100)**: Target is neither ambitious nor implausible, but lacks credibility due to undisclosed baseline, no secured supply evidence, and the broader contradiction posed by coal expansion.

### Key Finding

Ryanair's claim to power 12.5% of flights with SAF by 2030 is contradicted by documented active coal expansion (Urgewald Global Coal Exit List), prior regulatory enforcement for making misleading environmental claims (ASA 2020), an active European Commission investigation into the company's climate claims (2024), and obstructive climate policy lobbying (InfluenceMap D+). ### Data Gaps

- **EU ETS installation data**: No installation IDs found for Ryanair in the EU Emissions Trading System. This is not a gap for this assessment (aviation fuel usage is not tracked in EU ETS at the granularity needed to verify SAF percentage claims anyway), but it means SAF usage cannot be cross-checked against EU ETS fuel consumption data.

- **Intermediate SAF milestones**: Ryanair has not disclosed targets or milestones for SAF usage between 2026 and 2030. This prevents independent verification of progress and makes the 2030 target unverifiable until the final year.

- **SAF supply agreements**: No evidence found of binding offtake agreements or secured SAF supply for the claimed 12.5% by 2030. This gap is material—without supply commitments, the target lacks credibility.

- **Baseline SAF usage (2024–2025)**: Ryanair does not disclose current SAF usage as a percentage of total fuel consumption. This means the claimed 12.5% target cannot be evaluated against a known starting point and may represent minimal incremental progress.
